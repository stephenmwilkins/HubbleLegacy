Origins
Lyman Spitzer

The idea of a space observatory was first proposed by the American astronomer Lyman Spitzer shortly after the Second World War.

The Atmosphere

Spitzer explained that a space observatory could overcome the adverse effects of the Earth’s atmosphere. In particular this would enable observations at wavelengths which are normally absorbed such as X-rays, ultraviolet (UV), and infrared light.



Orbitting Solar Observatory

With the advent of space flight in the late 1950s this idea was soon realised.

The US’s Orbiting Solar Observatory programme and UK’s Ariel programme began in 1962. Both programmes observed the Sun (and eventually other objects) at wavelengths inaccessible from the ground.

Launch

Faulty Mirror

Soon after the launch of Hubble it was realised there was a serious problem. Hubble’s image quality was much worse than expected, potentially compromising much of the planned scientific analysis.


The problem was soon tracked to the mirror and it was realised that the mirror had the wrong shape.


However, Hubble was always designed to be serviceable and in 1993 Servicing Mission 1 installed a system, called to correct the problem. On the left shows an interactive comparison before and after the COSTAR system was installed.
