Introduction

One of the main - but not only - tools in an astronomers arsenal are astronomical observatories. Observertories typically serve the purpose of collecting more light (sensitivity) with greater sharpness (resolution) than possible by eye and creating a record (e.g. image) of that light.

Observatories are broadly defined by the combination of a telescope, instrumentation, and their location. All 3 can make a sigificant difference to how well an observatory performs.

The Atmosphere: astronomical seeing

Ground-based telescopes also suffer the effects of the Earth's atmosphere. In the ultraviolet and infrared this causes much of the light to be absorbed making astronomy at these wavelength impossible from the Earth's surface. In the visible the effect of the atmosphere is to distort and spread-out light making it difficult to see detail and observed faint objects.

Use the to turn on and off the effect of the Earth's atmosphere.

Resolution: telescopes and detectors

In order to record an image a telescope needs to use an instrument, typically an imaging camera or spectrograph. Visible light cameras capture light across a grid of pixels. Smaller pixels provide higher-resolution images but increase the noise in each individual pixels as the same amount of light is spread across multiple pixels. Smaller pixels also produce bigger files.

Use the sliders to control both the telescope resolution and the size of the pixels.

Sensitivity

Because astronomers are often observing very faint objects noise often becomes a major issue. Noise is reduced by collecting more light either by using a larger telescope or collecting light for a longer period of time, also know as integrating. To make its iconic Ultra Deep Field Hubble observed the same patch of sky for two million seconds (approximately 23 days).

Use the sliders to add noise to the image.
